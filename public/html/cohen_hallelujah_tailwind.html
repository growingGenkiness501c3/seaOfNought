🎼 Key & Meter

  • Key of C major (YouTube, Wikipedia).
  • 12/8 time signature, giving it a gentle triplet feel with a folk/gospel vibe.

🎸 Core Chord Progression

The famous verse progression is:

C – Am – C – Am – F – G – C – G

Numerically: I – vi – I – vi – IV – V – I – V (ClassicFM, RealWorldMusicTheory).

  • I–vi alternation (C ↔ Am): creates a subtle, hymn-like, devotional mood due to its tonal inertia (tonic to relative minor).
  • IV–V–I movement offers a classic cadential resolution—but Cohen often follows IV–V with vi, creating a deceptive resolution (“the minor fall”).

🧠 "The Fourth, the Fifth..." Lyric–Harmony Link

  • The lyrics describe the chord movement in real-time:
    • “the fourth” → IV chord (F major in C)
    • “the fifth” → V chord (G)
    • “the minor fall, the major lift” → vi chord (Am) followed by returning to IV

This is a self-referential musical moment where lyrics and harmony align beautifully.

💥 Secondary Dominants

  • Cohen introduces E7 (the V/vi, or V of Am), which intensifies the move into the vi chord—this is a text-book secondary dominant.

🎶 Melody & Counterpoint

  • Though harmonically simple, the melody adds depth through stepwise motion, occasional leaps, and counter-melodies.
  • Bob Dylan noted Cohen’s style uses gentle melodic contour with counterpoint, giving “a celestial character and melodic lift.”

⚖️ Emotional Texture

  • I–vi alternation creates a static, searching texture.
  • The deceptive resolution (IV–V–vi) evokes yearning and bittersweet surprise.
  • Secondary dominants add brief harmonic tension.

Cohen’s “Hallelujah” is a minimalist but emotionally rich piece. It thrives on:
